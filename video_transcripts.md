## Artificial Turf — Article 33

### Fall 2025 FATM Primer (Pre-recorded)

**Interview with Dr. Roger Luckmann, sponsor team** (Speaker 6)

**Position: For (moratorium/health concerns)**

Dr. Luckmann describes his background in occupational medicine, public health, and environmental health at UMass Medical School. He explains the growing concern about microplastics:

> "Microplastics are becoming an increasing concern in the medical community. We did have a similar motion before town meeting last spring. At that time, there was less information available about the health impacts of microplastics. But over the last year, there have been a couple of studies that have gotten me in particular very concerned."

> "We've identified in multiple studies that microplastics are present in all of our bodies, in all of our organs. Most recently it's been found in our brains. There is evidence now from the first clinical study — a study in the New England Journal of Medicine — that I think brings us to a different level in terms of the potential risk of microplastic in stroke and heart attack in particular."

On the scope of the moratorium:

> "In Natick, our moratorium is not about saying we need to take out our artificial turf we already have, or that we can't maintain it or resurface it. But we think it's time now, before we think about putting in any new artificial turf, to take a look at the evidence of the health impacts."

> "We could be headed to a place like we are with PFAS, where once we learn how widespread this is, how serious the health concerns are, we are going to be seeing a lot of regulation around artificial turf as a major contributor to microplastic."

### Oct 23, 2025 — Fall ATM Session 2

#### Sponsor Presents the Motion (Speaker 4 / Donna McKenzie)

**Position: For (moratorium)**

> "Move that town meeting adopt an advisory for all town departments to abide by a three year moratorium on the construction of artificial turf fields on town property for a period of three years from January 1st, 2026 to December 31st, 2028."

> "Our moratorium motion was referred to several town bodies. It's clear to us though that we do need a moratorium to allow the town to develop a comprehensive plan on the life cycle of artificial turf. A moratorium will ensure that the $2.2 million approved in the capital plan for field design and renovation will not be expended on artificial turf until we have a real consensus on this issue."

#### Dr. Roger Luckmann Presents Health Evidence (Speaker 4)

**Position: For (moratorium/health concerns)**

> "My focus tonight is on the health risks of these green plastic blades that make up the top layer of artificial turf. Specifically I'll be talking about the risks of microplastics and PFAS."

> "It's well established that artificial turf is a significant source of microplastic pollution of the air, soil, groundwater, waterways, and the ocean. Up to 8% of the blades are shed annually and break down into microplastic. For Toronto, that was an estimated 237 tons in one year."

> "Artificial turf has been shown to be 15% of all the plastic debris in the ocean off of Barcelona."

> "Laboratory studies and a growing body of clinical and epidemiologic research point to what I contend are probable serious human health effects from microplastic including cardiovascular disease, toxic effects on the liver, kidney, and other organs, as well as inflammation and damage to DNA."

> "Patients that had microplastic in their carotid artery plaque that was removed to improve their blood flow were 4.5 times more likely than those without microplastic in their arteries to have a stroke, heart attack, or to die during three years of follow up."

#### Tracy Stewart (Speaker 6) — Watershed and Legislative Concerns

**Position: For (moratorium)**

> "I am part of the Mount Sinai Turf Study Community Partners Group based out of New York and have witnessed the widespread and growing concern over artificial turf across Massachusetts for the past 15 years."

> "Natick has a prime opportunity to join this movement with a moratorium that provides time to assess field needs as Natick Public Schools has requested the status of the field exploration."

> "Article 33 aligns directly with state measures currently moving through the legislative process. Massachusetts resident support S.21807, which would ban state and municipal contracts for artificial turf. This bill was favorably moved this month to the House Senate Ways and Means Committee."

> "The Charles River Watershed Association opposes the installation of artificial turf as contrary to our core mission of promoting the health of the Charles River and its watershed."

> "Pennsylvania has become a dumping ground for artificial turf from Massachusetts. The Philadelphia Enquirer headline last month alarmingly said: 'recycle company's bankruptcy leaves tons of discarded artificial turf.' This highlights the difficulty of disposal after the use of eight to 10 years."

#### Anonymous Town Meeting Member (Speaker 7)

**Position: For (moratorium)**

> "Natick has a net zero plan. You have a sustainability director, you've banned plastic checkout bags and plastic straws. Some of your schools compost their plates."

#### Parent of Youth Athlete (Speaker unlabeled)

**Position: For (moratorium/personal experience)**

> "As a parent of a child who played on the Cole fields, the artificial turf field there, he hated it. It doesn't have to be a hundred degrees to be incredibly hot — it off-gasses. You can smell the gas coming off of it."

> "Because it doesn't drain naturally, when geese or other birds or animals poop on it, it kind of gets all wet and gross and the kids are playing in kind of a gross mix of feces."

> "I think there are a lot of reasons to not have more artificial turf and let's pause and do some of this additional good work and make a very thoughtful decision on what we do next."

#### Beverly Rich (Precinct 10) — Reading Statement for Martin Kessel, Chair of Open Space Advisory Committee

**Position: For (moratorium/cost concerns)**

> "Martin is chair of the Open Space Advisory Committee and would like to recall a two hour meeting the committee had with representatives from Rec and Park as well as the Natick Soccer Club in 2007 when the original decision was made to rebuild the Cole North soccer field, Natick's first artificial turf field. That was 18 years ago."

> "At that time open space was told that artificial turf was guaranteed for 15 to 20 years with $2,000 maintenance per year compared to $10,000 to $30,000 for grass. These 18-year-old numbers have changed significantly."

> "The FAQ document from the sponsors for the current Article 33 indicate that over a 16 year lifecycle artificial turf can be twice, even three times more expensive per field than natural grass when maintenance is included."

> "There was no mention in 2007 of the health and safety risks for artificial turf. These risks were apparently simply not known or perhaps thought to be insignificant. Tonight we've heard overwhelming evidence of these health and safety risks."

> "If even a fraction of this evidence turns out to be true, then I can tell you we would definitely not recommend and I urge you to vote yes for this moratorium."

#### Bruce Evans — Select Board Chair (Speaker unlabeled)

**Position: Neutral/For (structured study process)**

> "We reviewed this moratorium at a meeting about several weeks ago now, and our view as a board is that this proposed moratorium can serve as a catalyst for discussion."

> "This filing of Article 33 provides the opportunity to study synthetic turf versus grass issue as part of a much broader field use study with the study committee being formed to look at field usage and needs holistically."

> "The study committee can bring in all the experts, get the opinions, get the thoughts, have a reasonable discussion. That's why the three years matters. It's a suggested period to let us have a rational discussion as a town to figure out what's gonna be best."

#### Adam Boardman — Precinct 8 (Speaker 16)

**Position: Against/Neutral (concern about delay)**

> "I'm wondering why three years was chosen as a timeframe for the moratorium."

> "Would a delay of three years push that out of the five year plan? I do have a concern about that, that this might cause a delay in getting action taken and getting this field more usable for the public."

#### Natick Soccer Representative (Speaker unlabeled)

**Position: Against (field capacity concerns)**

> "Natick Soccer's lived experience using turf is a little bit different than what was presented. Certainly the temperatures have not been anything that have threatened use of the fields' drainage."

> "But the three year moratorium — definitely I am nervous about potentially pushing back any capital projects because the need is very real to us right now and we need to start making improvements to the field because we are in a good situation where we have some great youth sports programs and a lot of students who want to play, but we don't have the facilities currently to support that."

#### Town Meeting Member (Speaker 25)

**Position: For (moratorium/fiscal responsibility)**

> "I feel like if we vote to support this moratorium, we are telling the town we really do want to have a more full conversation about this. I think that it is the fiscally responsible thing to do. We've just had a $7 million override. We just approved $2.6 million in a feasibility study for Memorial."

#### Sarah Hannah — Precinct 8 (Speaker 26)

**Position: For (moratorium/precautionary principle)**

> "I spent my career in hazardous waste site assessment and cleanup and we as a society have this pattern where we discover something or find something or invent something that seems so useful and so wonderful that it's like a miracle and we use it everywhere. And only after a while do we realize that there's a downside to it."

> "Think DDT, PCBs, asbestos, PFAS, chlorinated solvents — the list goes on and on — they are wonderful and they are useful and they also have a downside. It takes a long time and money and cost to clean it up, witness our PFAS and groundwater. I think it behooves us to think about this carefully."

## Gas Leaf Blowers — Article 32

### Fall 2024 ATM — Session 3 (Sep 19, 2024)

**Presiding:** Frank W. Foss (Moderator)
**Sponsor:** Victoria Grafflin / EcoNatick (Speaker 17)
**Finance Committee:** No recommendation (Ms. Keeney / Speaker 3)
**Motion on floor:** Referral to Select Board, Board of Health, and sponsor (by Nancy North Graves, Precinct 6 / Speaker 13)

#### Moderator's Ruling on the Printed Motion

The Moderator ruled the positive motion printed in the Finance Committee recommendation book out of order:

> "I have ruled that motion out of order... nowhere in the text of that article does it warn you and the residents and the taxpayers and the interested parties in this town that you're gonna change a bylaw. Absent that, this motion that's printed in the book is out of scope — is beyond the scope of the article as it was presented and printed in the warrant."

#### Sponsor Presents the Proposal (Victoria Grafflin / EcoNatick, Speaker 17)

**Position: For (restriction/phase-out)**

Grafflin described a gradual transition away from gas-powered leaf blowers:

> "This is a gradual transition away from gas powered or internal combustion engine leaf blowers. What it's proposing is that starting in January, 2025, that gas powered leaf blowers only be able to be used during spring and fall cleanup periods, not during the summer for example. And that after two years in January, 2027, the use of gas powered leaf blowers would be banned with the following exemptions on properties above 1.5 acres, and during a time of emergency."

On enforcement:

> "The first offense would just be a written warning and then eventually after a third offense it would go up to $250 for a person, an individual, or $500 for a commercial property landscaper for example."

On the health rationale:

> "Air and noise pollution from gas powered lawn equipment, especially leaf blowers, creates unacceptable health risks to people, plants, and animals. The desire for hyper manicured landscapes should not outweigh the actual undocumented health risks created by the use of gas powered leaf blowers."

On what has changed:

> "There's a lot of new data. There was a report put out by US PIRG last year at this time that really documented just how bad and how polluting these gas powered leaf blowers are."

On the pollution data:

> "Most of the gas powered leaf blowers are two stroke engines, which are very inefficient. They combine the burning of gas and oil and they produce a lot of particulates. Particulates are not seen normally in cars anymore because we have things like catalytic converters."

On noise:

> "This is what most people think about is the noise pollution. Believe it or not, this is a hazard to your health."

On Middlesex County:

> "Middlesex County is actually the third most polluting county in terms of garden and lawn equipment. When you talk about particulate matter less than 2.5 microns, it's equivalent to almost 1.4 million cars."

On existing electric alternatives:

> "Current electric and battery powered leaf blowers are actually affordable. They really are practical. There's less maintenance required and they are significantly required and don't directly emit a plume of pollution into your neighborhood that's affecting your health."

On other municipalities:

> "There are at least 10 municipalities in Massachusetts who have taken some form of action on this. Some have older laws, some are brand new."

#### Jeff DeLuca — Precinct 6 (Speaker 19) — Opposition

**Position: Against (government overreach, fact-based concerns)**

DeLuca called the approach "dictation" rather than innovation:

> "If you truly want change to be a more green society within Natick, you can do it through innovation or dictation. This is dictation. That is not something that I want to be in my community. I would rather see innovation."

DeLuca challenged the noise comparison between gas and electric blowers with specific model data:

> "Facts are important and I wanna draw attention to a couple facts. Electric leaf blowers are not quiet. I own two of them and a gas one, I can tell you they're all loud. Battery powered EGO LB6504 blower tested at 92 decibels, while the most powerful Stihl backpack blower, a BR 800, tested at 78 decibels. So the electric or battery powered blower in this case is louder than the gas blower."

On commercial impact:

> "The subject matter and text of the article not only impacts Natick residents that own this current equipment, but also impacts commercial companies that service Natick residents, therefore also impacting Natick residents through increased costs or refusal of services."

#### Nancy North Graves — Precinct 6 (Speaker 13) — Motion Maker

**Position: For (referral / concerns about process)**

> "This article was not properly vetted. So no feedback was solicited from town administration. Input was not sought from local businesses, particularly our Natick based landscaping businesses, nor the public. And the language of the article has not been reviewed by town counsel."

#### Edward Devereaux — Precinct 8 — Support for the Concept

**Position: For (start the process)**

> "I commend the member who presented it for doing something that others have tried to do and haven't gotten it here. It belongs here."

#### Russell Gates — Lighthouse Landscape (Landscaper / Speaker 16)

**Position: Against (technology not ready)**

Gates detailed the cost and performance gap with current battery electric equipment:

> "The Stihl BGA 300 is $2,560 for the backpack, for one battery, for the charger. You want another battery? That's another $1,500 bucks."

On runtime:

> "Their claim to fame is that they can run 140 minutes on level one, which is a couple of leaves across your patio. Level three is gonna last a whopping 61 minutes for that one $1,500 battery. Now it's dead. So now we gotta put a generator in the nose of our trailers, which can echo off the trailer and the tailgate and now be louder than the blower we're using."

On the state of battery technology for commercial use:

> "These products are at least eight to 12 years away from being anything that's gonna be close to what we have."

On airflow comparison:

> "Our existing backpacks that we use right now... they blow 1,000 cubic feet per minute. The newest one that came out this year does 1,250 cubic feet per minute. This backpack right here [electric], 536 per minute."

#### Justin Samr — Johnny Bonsai Landscaping (Speaker 24)

**Position: Against (cost concerns)**

> "If these things cost this much... where does it end with... if they're gonna try to stop homeowners and businesses. What about municipalities? That impacts all of us, all our taxes, everything."

#### Eric Kaplan — Hemlock Drive / 40-year Landscaper (Speaker 25)

**Position: Against (commercial viability)**

> "Change is happening. I understand that for us as landscapers, the technology is not there. It just isn't ready yet for us to move to electric. The batteries only last an hour, an hour and a half. We can't run a business and do multiple houses in a day."

On his experience in towns with restrictions:

> "I work in a few other towns that have some of these bans and we can get maybe a half a day out of a battery at the most in the summertime. So technology's getting there, but it's just not there yet to be able to even in two years get to all electric."

#### Additional Support for Referral

**Ian alum, Precinct 9 (Speaker 21)** — Supported referral to allow proper process:

> "I think that we just heard the net zero report and how we need to move away from gas powered equipment and vehicles and we need to move towards electrification. So I think referral would be the proper avenue to do that so that the sponsor could confer with the departments and with the public and develop a working path forward."

**David Cressman, Precinct 10 (Speaker 16)** — Supported referral, noting innovation is happening:

> "We can put our heads in the sand if we want and say, yeah, gas is the way to go... I applaud the effort to start the process and I say, let's refer this. Let's let them do the research."

On his experience: he used his daughter's electric blower and found it "10 times as powerful."

| Motion | Yes | No | Abstain | Result |
